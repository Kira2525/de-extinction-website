The Journey to De-Extinction

The journey toward de-extinction wasn’t something that happened overnight. It’s a long story filled with major scientific breakthroughs, painful failures, and a lot of human curiosity that refused to quit. From the first time scientists successfully cloned a mammal to today’s bold attempts to bring back Ice Age animals.

1996: The Breakthrough That Started Everything

For a long time, de-extinction lived mostly in science fiction books and movies. Before scientists could even think about reviving extinct animals, they had to prove one basic thing first: that cloning mammals was actually possible. That proof came in 1996, when researchers at the Roslin Institute in Scotland stunned the world by cloning Dolly the sheep. Dolly became the first mammal ever cloned from an adult cell, and her birth changed the future of genetics forever.

Dolly the Sheep, born July 5, 1996, proved that cloning mammals was possible and opened the door to de-extinction research.

This wasn’t just some interesting science experiment. Dolly proved that DNA taken from an adult animal could be used to create a genetically identical copy, a real, living animal. That discovery was huge. It immediately raised a bigger question: if we can clone animals that are alive today, could we also clone animals that no longer exist?

The Dark Side of Success

Dolly’s story also came with an uncomfortable reality. She died at just six and a half years old from lung disease, even though sheep usually live 11 to 12 years. She also showed signs of aging much earlier than expected. This made scientists aware that cloning might work, but it may come with long-term health consequences.

Even with those concerns, Dolly changed everything. Her birth showed that cloning mammals was possible. Beth Shapiro said creating a true copy of an extinct species may never be possible. Any animals brought back would likely be stand-ins that fill similar ecological roles, not perfect replicas. Still, Dolly proved that the basic idea behind de-extinction wasn’t science fiction anymore.

2003: The First De-Extinction Experiment

After Dolly, scientists didn’t wait long to test the next big idea. In 2003, a team of researchers attempted something never done before, which was bringing back an extinct species. They cloned a Pyrenean ibex, also known as the bucardo, a mountain goat that had officially gone extinct in 2000.

This moment marked the first time an extinct animal was successfully brought back to life, even if only briefly. The cloned ibex was born alive but survived for just a few minutes before dying from a severe lung defect that made it impossible to breathe.

The bucardo’s short life made one thing painfully clear. The challenges of de-extinction weren’t hypothetical. Genetic gaps, physical deformities, and serious health problems were immediate and very real obstacles that could derail even the most carefully planned projects.

The Pyrenean ibex went extinct in 2000.

The bucardo experiment ended up raising far more questions than answers. While the cloning technically succeeded, the animal’s death highlighted just how risky this work really is. It forced scientists to confront serious issues like missing genetic information, long-term health problems, and whether revived animals could realistically survive at all. It became clear that de-extinction was nowhere near ready to move forward without much deeper research.

2013: De-Extinction Enters the Public Spotlight

For a long time after that, de-extinction research stayed mostly behind the scenes, happening quietly in labs with little public attention. That changed in February 2013, when entrepreneur and conservationist Stewart Brand brought the idea to a much wider audience during a TED conference in Long Beach, California.

Stewart Brand's groundbreaking 2013 TED Talk, "The Dawn of De-Extinction: Are You Ready?" introduced the concept to millions and fundamentally changed the public conversation about bringing back extinct species.

Brand’s talk became a major turning point in the de-extinction conversation. He argued that if humans played a role in driving certain species to extinction through hunting, habitat loss, and environmental damage, then we also carry some responsibility to try to repair that harm. His message went beyond genetics and technology. It focused on accountability, ethics, and whether we should try to make amends for the damage our species has caused.

The Brand Effect: After Brand’s TED Talk spread widely online and reached millions of viewers, public interest in de-extinction surged. Researchers found it easier to secure funding, discussions around extinction became more mainstream, and public perception began to shift. Extinction was no longer seen as strictly permanent, but as something science might be able to challenge.

Brand’s presentation helped show that de-extinction was not just a scientific possibility, but a potential opportunity. It sparked debates in classrooms, research institutions, and government circles worldwide. The conversation shifted from whether de-extinction could happen to when it might happen and what safeguards should be in place.

2017: CRISPR Changes Everything

In 2017, de-extinction research took another major step forward with the rapid adoption of CRISPR gene-editing technology. CRISPR, short for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats, allows scientists to edit DNA with a level of precision that wasn’t possible before.

CRISPR changed genetic engineering by letting scientists make precise, targeted edits to DNA, which made de-extinction feel far more realistic instead of just theoretical.

This tool changed everything by allowing scientists to edit DNA with a level of accuracy they’d never had before. Before CRISPR, genetic modification was more like trial and error, swapping pieces and hoping something worked. With CRISPR, researchers can target exact sections of DNA and change them intentionally. As Beth Shapiro explains, CRISPR makes it possible to take traits from extinct species and insert them into the DNA of their closest living relatives.

How CRISPR Works for De-Extinction

Take the woolly mammoth, for example. Scientists can’t rely on mammoth DNA alone because it’s too damaged and incomplete. Instead, CRISPR allows them to start with an Asian elephant, the mammoth’s closest living relative, and carefully edit its genes to add mammoth traits like thick fur, smaller ears, and adaptations for cold climates. The result wouldn’t be a true mammoth, but a hybrid that might survive in Arctic conditions and potentially fill the same ecological role.

Because CRISPR is both powerful and more affordable than older genetic tools, it quickly became one of the most important technologies driving de-extinction research. What once sounded like pure science fiction started to feel achievable. By around 2010, scientists were already seriously discussing bringing back species like the woolly mammoth, and by 2017, they finally had the tools to try.

2022-2025: The Race Accelerates

2022 - The Tasmanian Tiger Project

In August 2022, researchers at the University of Melbourne announced plans to revive one of Australia’s most well-known extinct animals, the Tasmanian tiger, also called the thylacine. This marsupial predator was last seen alive in 1936, when the final known individual died in captivity at the Hobart Zoo.

The comprehensive roadmap for bringing back the Tasmanian tiger includes genetic sequencing, embryo development, and ecological planning.

One reason the thylacine is considered a strong candidate for de-extinction is how recently it went extinct. Kevin Maimann points out that because it disappeared less than 90 years ago, researchers still have access to relatively well-preserved DNA from museum collections and other samples. That said, the project isn’t straightforward. Thylacines were marsupials, meaning they carried their young in pouches, so scientists would need to develop artificial pouches or identify suitable surrogate species to support early development.

As the project moved forward, the cost of de-extinction became impossible to ignore. Estimates quickly climbed into the tens of millions of dollars, reigniting debates about priorities. Many conservationists argued that this funding would be better spent protecting species that are already endangered rather than trying to revive ones that are already gone.

2023 - The Woolly Mammoth Project Officially Launches

Scientists had discussed the idea of reviving the woolly mammoth for decades, but by 2023, advances in tools like CRISPR finally made serious planning possible. That year, Colossal Biosciences officially launched the Woolly Mammoth Revival Project, backed by substantial private investment and ambitious timelines.

A scientifically accurate reconstruction showing what a genetically engineered woolly mammoth could look like using modern DNA editing techniques.

Scientists have been analyzing mammoth DNA recovered from frozen remains in Siberian permafrost and comparing it to the DNA of Asian elephants, the mammoth’s closest living relative. Using CRISPR, the goal is to edit elephant cells to include mammoth genes, ultimately creating an elephant–mammoth hybrid with key mammoth traits.

Kevin Maimann points out that if research keeps moving at its current pace, scientists believe a woolly mammoth could potentially be brought back as early as 2028. That’s not some distant future, it’s within our lifetime. It means children born today could grow up in a world where mammoths exist again, not just in museums or textbooks.

2025 - The Return of the Dire Wolf

Two dire wolf pups represent the promise and controversy of de-extinction, raising questions about what it truly means to bring an extinct species back to life.

In early 2025, scientists made headlines with a dramatic claim: they had successfully recreated the dire wolf using genetic material from gray wolves and domestic dogs. People from around the world were excited about the return of this Ice Age predator.

As soon as the announcement hit the news, the backlash started. Scientists, conservationists, and ethicists were quick to point out that these animals weren’t actually dire wolves. Critics argued that what had been created were genetically altered gray wolves, not true revivals of an extinct species. Alexandre Azevedo and Manuel Magalhães-Sant'Ana later published an in-depth analysis that dug into the welfare concerns, legal gray areas, and identity confusion surrounding the project.

The debate opened up some important questions. What actually makes an animal “real”? If something is made up of mostly dire wolf DNA but still relies on gray wolf genes, what do we call it? Is it a dire wolf, a wolf hybrid, or an entirely new species? And under current wildlife laws, how should animals like this even be protected? These questions don’t have clear answers yet, and experts are still arguing about them.

The controversial 2025 dire wolf revival sparked heated debates about what qualifies as "true" de-extinction versus creating hybrid animals.

The Complete De-Extinction Timeline

1996 - Dolly the Sheep

Dolly becomes the first mammal cloned from an adult cell at Scotland’s Roslin Institute, proving cloning was possible and setting the stage for future de-extinction ideas.

2003 - Pyrenean Ibex Revival

The first extinct species is briefly brought back to life. The cloned bucardo survives only minutes due to lung defects, showing how risky de-extinction is.

2013 - TED Talk Revolution

Stewart Brand’s Ted Talk went viral pushes de-extinction into the public spotlight, changing how people think about extinction and attracting funding and attention.

2017 - CRISPR Game-Changer

CRISPR gene-editing technology changes everything, making it possible to edit DNA with precision and turning de-extinction into something scientists could realistically attempt.

2022 - Tasmanian Tiger Project

The University of Melbourne announces plans to revive the thylacine using preserved DNA, sparking renewed debate about ethics, funding, and priorities.

2023 - Woolly Mammoth Project

Colossal Biosciences officially launches its mammoth project, predicting a possible return by 2028 using Asian elephant DNA.

2025 - Dire Wolf Returns

Gene-edited wolves resembling dire wolves are announced, igniting global debate over whether this counts as real de-extinction or something completely different

What Comes Next?

We’re standing at a turning point. With multiple projects moving fast and technology advancing even faster, we may soon have to answer questions no one has faced before. What happens when extinct animals return? Who is responsible for them? And how do we make sure we don’t repeat the same mistakes that caused their extinction in the first place?
