The Extent of the Problem: Why De-Extinction is Harder Than It Looks

De-extinction can sound pretty straightforward: recover some DNA, clone it, and bring an extinct species back. In reality, it’s anything but simple. These projects are incredibly complex, costly, and packed with ethical concerns. Every attempt at de-extinction runs into serious technical hurdles, biological limits, and real-world challenges that can easily undermine even the most ambitious plans.

An overview of de-extinction science, illustrating how DNA preservation, time since extinction, and available technology affect whether extinct species could realistically be brought back.

Problem #1: They Wouldn’t Truly Be the Original Species

Here’s a hard truth that often gets brushed aside in de-extinction discussions: no matter how advanced the technology becomes, it’s still new, experimental, and full of unknowns. One of the biggest issues is that any animal brought back through de-extinction would not be an exact copy of the species that originally went extinct.

The DNA Breakdown Issue

DNA doesn’t last forever. Even when remains are frozen in permafrost or exceptionally well preserved, genetic material slowly breaks down over time. The older the specimen, the more damaged and incomplete the DNA becomes. Scientists can only work with what’s left, which means critical pieces of the genetic code are often missing.

Think of it like trying to restore a centuries-old book when large sections are missing and the remaining pages are smudged and torn. You can make educated guesses using similar books, but you’ll never recreate the original word for word. That’s exactly the challenge scientists face with extinct DNA.

To fill in the gaps, researchers often rely on DNA from related living species. That means the result isn’t a true revival, but a hybrid, part extinct animal, part modern relative. Beth Shapiro, a leading researcher in ancient DNA, has pointed out that these animals are better described as “proxies” or stand-ins. They may resemble the original species and behave similarly, but genetically, they are not the same.

This leads to an uncomfortable question: if an animal is mostly mammoth but partially elephant, is it really a mammoth? Or is it something entirely new? And if it’s not truly the original species, does bringing it back still accomplish what de-extinction claims to promise?

Problem #2: Serious Health Issues

An even more troubling concern is the strong likelihood that de-extinct animals would suffer from significant health problems. Missing DNA, patched genetic sequences, and artificial manipulation create a ripple effect of medical risks that could follow these animals throughout their lives.

Scientists predict a wide range of potential health complications, including:

  • Physical abnormalities that interfere with movement, feeding, or basic survival.
  • Compromised immune systems that make animals highly vulnerable to modern diseases.
  • Developmental issues that may not appear until later stages of life.
  • Accelerated aging, similar to what was observed in early cloning experiments.
  • Reproductive difficulties that could prevent healthy populations from forming.

So even if a de-extinct animal survives birth, which is already a major hurdle, there’s no guarantee it would live a long or healthy life. Instead, it could experience chronic illness, pain, and disability with limited options for treatment or relief.

The Lesson from Dolly the Sheep

Dolly the sheep was expected to live over a decade, but she died at just 6.5 years old. She developed arthritis unusually early and suffered from chronic lung disease. Scientists still debate whether cloning caused these issues, but her shortened and difficult life highlights the risks involved when we manipulate biology in ways we don’t fully understand.

Problem #3: Today’s World Is Nothing Like the Past

Even if scientists were able to create physically healthy de-extinct animals, those animals would be born into an environment completely unlike the one their species evolved in. Modern ecosystems have shifted dramatically, and introducing long-lost species could disrupt those systems in unpredictable ways.

The future of our planet depends on the choices we make today, destruction or restoration.

Environments today are fundamentally different from what they were when extinct animals originally lived. Consider the changes that have occurred:

Major Environmental Changes:

  • Climate change has altered temperature ranges, seasonal patterns, and weather extremes.
  • Habitat loss means there's dramatically less wild space available.
  • Food sources that sustained extinct species may no longer exist or may be different nutritionally.
  • Natural predators are often extinct themselves or have evolved different hunting strategies.
  • Competition from species that filled the ecological niche after extinction.

The passenger pigeon once lived in forests that covered millions of acres. Today, those forests are fragmented and fundamentally different ecosystems.

De-extinct species might not have any natural predators left in today’s world, which could let their populations grow out of control and overwhelm local ecosystems. On the flip side, they could run into entirely new predators or diseases their species never evolved to handle. And then there’s food. What if modern environments can’t meet their nutritional needs? What if the plants they once relied on have changed, become toxic, or simply don’t exist in large enough amounts anymore? In that case, these animals could starve and go extinct all over again, this time in front of cameras and social media.

The Mammoth Dilemma

Take the woolly mammoth as an example. Mammoths evolved during the Ice Age, surviving in cold tundra environments with very specific plants, temperatures, and seasonal cycles. Today’s Arctic is warming fast, permafrost is melting, and vegetation is shifting. Even if scientists manage to create a mammoth, elephant hybrid, the big question remains: where would it actually live? The world it was built for no longer exists in the same way.

Problem #4: Legal and Regulatory Gaps

Another major issue is that our current laws were never designed with de-extinct species in mind. There’s no clear legal roadmap for how these animals should be treated, which leaves dangerous consequences.

Here are just a few of the legal questions that still don’t have clear answers:

  • Who owns de-extinct animals? The company that created them? Governments? The public? Or no one at all?
  • How should they be protected? Do endangered species laws apply, or are they excluded?
  • Who is responsible if something goes wrong? If a revived predator attacks someone or damages property, who is held accountable?
  • How are they classified legally? Are they endangered, experimental, or an entirely new category?
  • Can they be patented? If a corporation spends millions creating a species, do they own it?

Sound the Alarm

As far back as 2017, a research team led by Norman Wagner warned in the journal Science that wildlife laws and legal definitions urgently need updates to account for de-extinct species. Without clear legal frameworks, releasing these animals could lead to regulatory chaos. Years later, most of those laws still haven’t been revised.

Wildlife protection laws were built on the assumption that extinction is permanent. De-extinction challenges that idea, and our legal systems simply haven’t caught up yet. That uncertainty creates risks not just for the animals, but also for researchers, governments, and the public.

Problem #5: The Funding Dilemma

De-extinction expensive. These projects can cost millions of dollars, covering years of research, advanced technology, specialized facilities, veterinary care, and long-term monitoring.

Many conservation experts argue that funding should prioritize protecting living species and habitats.

Many conservation biologists say that this money would be far better spent saving species that are still alive and can still be protected. Thousands of species are already on the edge of extinction including elephants, rhinos, tigers, gorillas, orangutans, birds, amphibians, fish, and insects are disappearing at an alarming rate.

The Opportunity Cost

For perspective, the estimated $15 million it might take to bring back one woolly mammoth could instead fund protection programs for dozens of endangered species, preserve thousands of acres of habitat, or support anti-poaching efforts for years. Is reviving one extinct species really worth losing several living ones?

If conservation funding is redirected toward de-extinction, more living species will likely go extinct while we wait to see if de-extinction even works. That creates a revolving-door problem where we lose species faster than we can bring them back.

Economist Kevin Maimann says that while de-extinction is becoming more technically possible, it raises serious concerns about resource allocation and the long-term cost to biodiversity that is already under threat.

Problem #6: The Corporate Profit Motive

There’s also a less comfortable possibility that doesn’t get talked about enough: some companies may see de-extinction as a business opportunity rather than a conservation effort.

Instead of focusing on science or ecosystem health, revived species could be turned into:

  • Tourist attractions marketed as spectacles, regardless of animal welfare.
  • Commercial products like ivory, fur, or branded merchandise.
  • Marketing tools used to improve corporate images without real conservation impact.
  • Entertainment assets for zoos, private collections, or even hunting reserves.

The "Jurassic Park" Problem

Science fiction warned us about this for a reason. When profit becomes the main driver, animal welfare and ecosystem health tend to come last. If de-extinction turns into a business venture, suffering and exploitation are almost guaranteed.

If profit takes priority, de-extinction risks becoming more about spectacle than responsibility. In that scenario, both the animals and the environment pay the price.

The Bottom Line

These aren’t small or hypothetical concerns. They’re serious, foundational issues that question whether de-extinction should move forward at all. Without honest conversations, strong safeguards, and clear priorities, we risk spending enormous resources only to create suffering animals, damaged ecosystems, and yet another extinction.
